Energy-Equivalence Model — Mathematical Background

This page describes the physics and mathematics behind the Dynamic Energy-Equivalence Modeling tool for rowing. The core insight is that two boats travelling at the same average speed can require very different amounts of metabolic energy depending on how smoothly that speed is maintained across each stroke.

2. Power–Velocity Relationship

A boat moving through water experiences a drag force approximately proportional to the square of its velocity:

$$ F_{\text{drag}}(v) = k \, v^2 $$

The propulsive power required to overcome this drag is force times velocity:

$$ P(v) = F_{\text{drag}} \cdot v = k \, v^3 $$

This cubic relationship is the physical foundation of the model. Doubling boat speed requires eight times the power.

3. Energy Per Stroke

The mechanical energy expended during one stroke cycle of duration \(\tau\) is:

$$ E = \int_0^{\tau} P(t) \, dt = k \int_0^{\tau} v(t)^3 \, dt $$

The mean power over the stroke is therefore:

$$ \bar{P} = \frac{E}{\tau} = \frac{k}{\tau} \int_0^{\tau} v(t)^3 \, dt $$

Because the stroke phase \(\phi = t / \tau\) is used internally, this becomes:

$$ \bar{P} = k \int_0^{1} v(\phi)^3 \, d\phi $$

In the tool, this integral is approximated as the arithmetic mean of \(v(\phi_i)^3\) across the sampled phase points.

4. Why Velocity Variation Matters

Because \(f(v) = v^3\) is a strictly convex function, Jensen's inequality states:

$$ \overline{v^3} \;\geq\; \bar{v}^3 $$

That is, the mean of the cubed velocities is always greater than or equal to the cube of the mean velocity, with equality only when \(v(t)\) is constant. Any intra-stroke speed fluctuation therefore increases \(\bar{P}\) above the theoretical minimum \(k\,\bar{v}^3\) required to sustain that average speed.

Practical implication: A boat that accelerates and decelerates dramatically within each stroke — such as one where the catch is mistimed or the recovery is rushed — must produce more total power than one that glides smoothly, even when both cover the same distance in the same time.

5. Finish-Time Estimation

For a boat moving at constant speed, \(P \propto v^3\) and \(v = d/T\), so \(P \propto T^{-3}\), which gives \(T \propto P^{-1/3}\). Applying this ratio between two crews who share the same energy budget:

$$ \frac{T_B}{T_A} = \left(\frac{\bar{P}_B}{\bar{P}_A}\right)^{1/3} $$
$$ \boxed{T_B = T_A \times \left(\frac{\bar{P}_B}{\bar{P}_A}\right)^{1/3}} $$

If Crew B's velocity profile requires more mean power than Crew A's for the same average speed (\(\bar{P}_B > \bar{P}_A\)), then \(T_B > T_A\): Crew B would finish later given an identical physiological energy budget.

6. Curve Normalisation

When Crew B's curve is drawn, it is rescaled so that its arithmetic mean velocity matches Crew A's:

$$ v_B^{\text{norm}}(\phi) = v_B(\phi) \times \frac{\bar{v}_A}{\bar{v}_B} $$

This preserves the shape of the drawn profile (the pattern of acceleration and deceleration across the stroke) while ensuring the comparison is made on equal average-speed terms. Only after normalisation is \(\bar{P}_B\) computed for the finish-time estimate.

7. Stroke Rate and Power-Velocity Efficiency

Stroke rate is an important variable in rowing — higher rates affect muscle recruitment, blade mechanics, and pacing strategy. However, stroke rate has no effect on power-velocity efficiency as modelled here, provided the shape of the velocity curve (the pattern of acceleration and deceleration expressed as a function of normalised stroke phase \(\phi\)) remains the same.

To see why, note that the mean power integral (Section 3) is taken over the normalised phase \(\phi \in [0,1]\), which stretches or compresses time to fill exactly one stroke regardless of how long that stroke lasts:

$$ \bar{P} = k \int_0^{1} v(\phi)^3 \, d\phi $$

The stroke duration \(\tau = 60 / r\) does not appear in this expression. Changing the rate while keeping the normalised shape identical simply rescales the time axis uniformly, leaving the phase-domain integral — and therefore \(\bar{P}\) and the finish-time estimate — unchanged.

Implication: What matters to efficiency is the shape of the velocity fluctuation within a stroke — how smoothly speed is maintained — not how quickly the stroke cycle repeats. Two crews rowing at different rates but with identical normalised velocity profiles will incur the same energy penalty relative to steady-state propulsion.

8. Energy Penalty

The energy penalty quantifies how much extra energy Crew B expends relative to Crew A to cover the full 2000 m race:

$$ \Delta E\% = \frac{E_B - E_A}{E_A} \times 100\% = \left(\frac{\bar{P}_B}{\bar{P}_A} - 1\right) \times 100\% $$

A positive penalty means Crew B's velocity profile is less efficient; a negative value (possible if Crew B's curve is smoother than Crew A's) means Crew B could sustain the same pace on less energy.

10. Implementation Constants

ConstantValueDescription
DRAG_COEFFICIENT \((k)\) 50.0 kg/m Hydrodynamic drag coefficient calibrated for a rowing shell. Relates drag force to velocity squared: \(F = k\,v^2\), and therefore power to velocity cubed: \(P = k\,v^3\). This value is representative of a single scull; heavier boats have higher \(k\).
RACE_DISTANCE \((d)\) 2000 m Standard Olympic and World Championship rowing race distance. Used to convert average velocity to finish time: \(T = d / \bar{v}\).

Why \(k\) does not affect the result

Although \(k\) appears in the power formula, it cancels completely in the finish-time estimate and energy penalty because both quantities depend only on the ratio of mean powers:

$$ \frac{\bar{P}_B}{\bar{P}_A} = \frac{k \displaystyle\int_0^{1} v_B(\phi)^3\,d\phi} {k \displaystyle\int_0^{1} v_A(\phi)^3\,d\phi} = \frac{\displaystyle\int_0^{1} v_B(\phi)^3\,d\phi} {\displaystyle\int_0^{1} v_A(\phi)^3\,d\phi} $$

The absolute value of \(k\) is therefore irrelevant to the comparison; only the shape of the velocity profiles matters.

Numerical integration

The energy integral is evaluated using the trapezoidal rule across the sampled phase points \(\phi_0, \phi_1, \ldots, \phi_n\):

$$ E \approx k \sum_{i=0}^{n-1} \frac{v(\phi_i)^3 + v(\phi_{i+1})^3}{2} \,(\phi_{i+1} - \phi_i) $$

The reference curve is sampled at approximately 100 phase points per stroke, providing sufficient resolution for the trapezoidal approximation to be accurate.
